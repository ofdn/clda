## Module 1
### Planning before documenting

---

## Trainer Goal

Help participants plan from their own lives and communities, not from outside.

By the end, each participant should have a **simple, written plan** covering:

- What is missing and needs documentation
- Who they will work with
- What they will document and why it matters
- Where the recording might live long-term

---

## 1.1 Which topics are missing

*Trainer: Ganesh Birua*

Ask participants to think about their own village, town, or community and list:

- Topics not yet recorded — a ritual, festival, farming or forest work, a local issue, a craft, women's work, migration
- Elders and others who hold important knowledge, songs, or stories
- Who holds power between the documenter and the interviewee?
- Places that carry memory — a river, shrine, meeting place, market, forest path

> "What do we know is important, but we do not yet have in audio or video?"

___

### 1.1 Exercise

Ask participants to write down **3 things**:

1. A topic or area that is not yet documented but should be
2. One or two people you already know who can speak about it
3. A place where you might record them

---

## 1.2 Who to interview and how

*Trainer: Ganesh Birua*

How documenters identify people to interview:

- **Asking around** — who is known for songs, stories, rituals, farming knowledge, or memories?
- **Listening** in meetings, festivals, or everyday conversations to see who explains things clearly and with care

___

### 1.2 Approaching people respectfully

- **First contact** — visit, phone call, or message to introduce yourself and explain why you are interested
- **Meeting** — sit with them, explain what you are doing, who you are doing it with, and what will happen to the recording

It is fine if someone says **no** or wants to think.  
Planning includes giving people **time and choice**.

---

## 1.3 Planned vs On-the-go recording

*Trainer: Ramjit Tudu*

Documentation happens in two ways — both are valid:

| Type | When |
|------|------|
| **Planned visits** | Documenter fixes a time and place in advance |
| **On-the-go** | Documenter sees or hears something important while already somewhere — a field, festival, bus, protest |

___

### 1.3 Tips for on-the-go recording

- Keep your phone or recorder **charged** with free space
- Make a **quick note** of date, place, and names immediately after
- Think of on-the-go recordings as **part of the plan**, not as mistakes

> "If you see or hear something important and safe to record, it is better to record quickly and follow up later with a calmer, planned conversation."

---

## 1.4 Where will recordings live?

*Trainer: Ramjit Tudu*

Three types of "homes" for recordings:

1. **Community spaces** — local groups, libraries, youth clubs, schools, community centres
2. **Partner platforms** — organisations such as PARI and archives that work closely with communities
3. **Large platforms and archives** — Wikimedia projects, regional or national archives, specialist archives

___

### 1.4 What to prioritise

- **Community spaces come first** — the first responsibility is to the people whose voices and images are recorded
- External archives and large platforms can be added **later**, after consent is clear and people are comfortable
- **Discuss sensitive topics** with the community — record but give access only to the community

Ask participants:
- "If I record this, where in my community can people listen or watch it later?"
- "Which partner organisation or platform could be a good second home, if any?"

---

## 1.5 Consent, Safety, and Labour

*Trainer: Shalini A*

Three questions to connect planning to action:

1. **Consent** — "How will I explain clearly what I am doing and where the recording may go?"
2. **Safety** — "Could this recording put anyone at risk if it is shared? How can I reduce that risk?"
3. **Labour** — "Who is giving their time and knowledge? How will we recognise and compensate this work?"

___

### 1.5 What is consent?

An agreement or permission to engage in an activity — a safe, open, comfortable conversation between all parties.

Consent must be:
- **Affirmative** — clear words or actions
- **Voluntary** — given freely, without pressure
- **Informed** — person understands what will happen
- **Specific** — for this purpose, not everything
- **Withdrawable** — at any point, by informing each party

___

### 1.5 Types of consent

| Type | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Implied** | Given by a person's action or gesture |
| **Express** | Directly and clearly given with explicit words |
| **Informed** | Fully informed process, often with a signed document |
| **Unanimous** | Complete agreement among multiple parties |

___

### 1.5 Recording consent

**What it means:**
- Permission to officially capture and store audio, video, and images
- Communities or individuals must be **fully aware** of how their knowledge will be recorded, stored, and used
- **Ownership** — ensure recordings remain with the knowledge holder

**Audience question:**  
*What if the information is already used by others and they want to withdraw it?*

___

### 1.5 Safety risks

Sharing private, confidential, or non-consensual recordings can cause:

- Legal consequences, reputational damage, and harm to emotional wellbeing
- Harassment, bullying, blackmail, or public shame
- Employment troubles
- Physical safety risks — exposing personal identity, location, enabling stalking
- Misinformation — media manipulated or clipped out of context
- Data breaches — sensitive information exposed permanently

___

### 1.5 Reducing safety risks

- **Evaluate first** — is sharing absolutely necessary?
- **Know your audience** — who can access the data and what will they use it for?
- **Use secure channels** — implement strong security practices
- **Anonymize** — mask names, locations, voices, blur faces where needed
- **Restrict access** — share on secure, private platforms rather than publicly
- **Delete after use** — remove recordings and copies when no longer needed
- **Watermarks** — can prohibit redistribution
- *Legally, you can invoke Right to Privacy under Article 21*

___

### 1.5 Labour — who contributes?

**Who gives time and knowledge:**
- Individuals or groups from the community being documented
- Individuals or groups who do the documenting

**Who organises and travels:**
- **Elders and knowledge keepers** — preserve and pass down community knowledge
- **Researchers and institutions** — local and global, formal documentation
- **Regional NGOs** — train community members, organise and publish local knowledge (e.g. Keystone Foundation)

___

### 1.5 Recognition and compensation

- **Respect ownership** — the community owns their data
- **Secure consent** before documenting, recording, or publishing their knowledge
- **Always credit** specific individuals and communities — do not generalise to anonymous groups
- **Fair compensation** — direct financial remuneration, community capacity building, employing community members as paid researchers, project leaders, and co-authors — not just subjects

---

## Trainer Activity

For their first few planned recordings, ask participants to note:

1. Who will be the speaker(s)?
2. Who will accompany or help during the recording?
3. Any worries they already see — sensitive topics, borders, land conflict, gender-based risk

These notes will be used again in **Module 2** when they learn specific ways to record consent, decide access levels, and think about payment.
