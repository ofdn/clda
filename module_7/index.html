    How to share materials with archives, libraries, and communities

    Key points: How to contact archives and libraries and deposit media for archiving. How to share copies with the community. How to plan follow-up support after the workshop. Trainer goal Help participants understand where their materials can live in the long term and how to share them safely and respectfully. By the end, each participant should know at least one archive they could approach, and have a simple plan for sharing copies with their community after the workshop. 7.1 Types of places that can hold your materials Give examples of where language materials can be kept: Preservation on websites, social media, privately (most cases). Community archives (run by a local organisation or group). Public libraries, university libraries, and cultural organisations. Digital language archives (examples: AILLA, ELAR, Kaipuleohone). Wikimedia projects (Commons, Wikidata, Wikipedia) as public access layers. Explain that different places have different rules: Some archives take only certain formats or languages. Some require deposit agreements or specific licenses. Some community archives prefer “post-custodial” models: communities own materials legally, institution supports storage and access. 7.2 How to contact an archive or library Encourage participants to contact archives early. Suggest basic steps for first contact: Find an email or contact form for the archive or library. Introduce who you are and what community you work with. Explain the type of materials you have (audio, video, photos, text) and in which language(s). Ask if they are open to community deposits or partnerships. Suggest what information to include: Project or collection title. Languages and place. Types and formats of files (for example, .wav, .mp4, .jpg). Rough amount of data (number of files or approximate size). Any access limits or consent conditions. 7.3 Preparing for deposit Explain how institutional archives and libraries will ask for: A basic description of the collection (who, where, when, what). A list of files with titles and dates. Information about language(s) and people involved. Clear statements about rights, consent, and access levels. Show how participants’ existing work can help: Their folder structure and file naming make it easier to share. Their metadata logs can be adapted into a deposit form or catalogue entry. Their consent notes and access ladder help archives understand restrictions. Discuss with archive/library for “progressive deposit”: They can send small batches over time, instead of waiting for a “perfect” collection. This approach appears in several language archiving recommendations. 7.4 Sharing copies with the community Stress that community sharing is as important as formal deposit: Community members should be able to listen, watch, and use the materials if they wish. Suggest practical ways to share copies: Local listening or viewing sessions. Copies on USB drives or memory cards for community elders or schools. Closed-access WhatsApp or Telegram groups for sharing selected clips. Printed transcriptions or booklets for schools or community centres. Encourage a feedback loop: Ask community members what is useful and what feels sensitive. Adjust future documentation and sharing based on this feedback.
